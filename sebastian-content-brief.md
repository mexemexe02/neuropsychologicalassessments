### What is a neuropsychological assessment?
"A neuropsychological assessment examines how different areas of thinking and behaviour are functioning."

Areas explored:
- Attention and concentration
- Learning and memory
- Language and communication
- Visual and spatial abilities
- Processing speed
- Reasoning and problem-solving
- Planning and organization
- Academic skills
- Emotional and behavioural functioning
- Adaptive and everyday living skills

"Results are interpreted in the context of the individual's developmental, educational, medical, psychological, social, and cultural background."

### Reasons for seeking an assessment
(Each as a card/section)

1. Attention and executive functioning — "Difficulties with concentration, organization, planning, task completion, time management, impulsivity, or managing multiple responsibilities."

2. Learning and academic performance — "Persistent challenges involving reading, writing, mathematics, comprehension, studying, or retaining newly learned information."

3. Memory and cognitive changes — "Concerns about forgetfulness, confusion, reduced mental efficiency, difficulty finding words, or changes in everyday functioning."

4. Developmental differences — "Questions about developmental progress, communication, behaviour, social functioning, adaptive skills, or differences in how a person learns and processes information."

5. Medical or neurological conditions — "Changes associated with a neurological condition, medical illness, brain injury, stroke, epilepsy, cancer treatment, chronic illness, or another health-related concern."

6. Emotional and behavioural functioning — "Concerns involving anxiety, mood, emotional regulation, behaviour, motivation, or the interaction between emotional wellbeing and cognitive performance."

7. Diagnostic clarification — "A need to better understand whether an individual's difficulties may be associated with ADHD, a learning disorder, an intellectual or developmental condition, a neurocognitive disorder, or another psychological or neurological concern."

8. Planning and recommendations — "A need for individualized recommendations related to school, postsecondary education, employment, treatment, rehabilitation, independent living, or other supports."

### Understanding Neurodivergent Conditions
"Different minds process the world in different ways.

Neurodiversity recognizes that there is natural variation in how people think, learn, communicate, focus, regulate emotions, and experience their environments.

The term neurodivergent is commonly used to describe individuals whose neurological development or cognitive functioning differs from what is considered typical. This may include individuals with:
- Attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder, or ADHD
- Autism spectrum disorder
- Specific learning disorders
- Intellectual or developmental disabilities
- Tourette syndrome and tic disorders
- Developmental language or communication differences
- Other cognitive or sensory-processing differences

Some people identify strongly with the term neurodivergent, while others prefer diagnostic or person-specific language. We respect each individual's preferred terminology.

Neurodivergence is not defined only by difficulty.

A neurodivergent person may experience meaningful strengths alongside areas requiring support.

These may include strengths in creativity, focused interests, visual thinking, problem-solving, honesty, pattern recognition, memory for specific information, persistence, or alternative ways of understanding complex ideas.

At the same time, the individual may experience difficulties with attention, sensory demands, transitions, communication, emotional regulation, social expectations, academic tasks, organization, or daily routines.

The goal of assessment is to understand this individual pattern rather than compare the person to a single idea of how everyone should function."

### How an assessment may help
- Identify cognitive and academic strengths
- Clarify areas that require support
- Explore whether diagnostic criteria are met
- Distinguish between conditions with overlapping symptoms
- Understand how emotional, developmental, medical, and environmental factors may affect functioning
- Explain why certain tasks or environments are especially difficult
- Identify strategies that align with the individual's learning and processing style
- Support requests for appropriate accommodations
- Guide educational, vocational, psychological, or medical planning
- Improve self-understanding and reduce unhelpful self-blame

"A diagnosis may be one outcome of an assessment, but it is not the only purpose. The broader objective is to provide an accurate and useful understanding of the individual's functioning."

### A neuro-affirming approach
"We approach neurodivergent functioning with curiosity, respect, and clinical care.

This means that we seek to understand both the challenges a person experiences and the strengths, adaptations, and perspectives they bring. Recommendations are intended to support functioning and wellbeing without asking the person to suppress important aspects of their identity.

When appropriate, recommendations may address environmental changes, communication preferences, sensory needs, learning strategies, executive-function supports, emotional wellbeing, and self-advocacy."

### Who may benefit

### Preparing for a neuropsychological assessment
- Review appointment details
- Take prescribed medications as usual
- Bring glasses, hearing aids, mobility supports
- Sleep and eat before
- Bring medication list and medical info
- Bring previous reports, school records
- Let practice know about accessibility/sensory needs
- Avoid alcohol/non-prescribed substances before testing

### Preparing a child or adolescent
- Use simple language, avoid "exam" framing
- Bring comfort items, snacks
- Share sensory/anxiety info beforehand

### Documents that may be helpful
Previous assessments, medical reports, medication list, school records, IEPs, workplace documentation

- Private quiet space for virtual sessions
